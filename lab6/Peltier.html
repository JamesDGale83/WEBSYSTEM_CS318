Thermoelectric Effect

The thermoelectric effect is the direct conversion between heat and electricity, encompassing the Seebeck effect (generating electricity from heat), the Peltier effect (heating or cooling from electricity), and the Thomson effect (heat absorption or production along a conductor with a current and temperature gradient). This phenomenon involves the diffusion of charge carriers from hotter to colder regions, creating a voltage, and is utilized in devices for power generation from waste heat and for cooling or heating applications, such as in portable refrigerators and electronic cooling.

Peltier cooler

A Peltier cooler, or thermoelectric cooler, works by using the Peltier effect, where a direct current passing through two different semiconductor materials causes one side to absorb heat and become cold, while the other side emits heat and becomes hot. Heat is then removed from the hot side using a heat sink and fan, effectively pumping it away from the cold side. Because it has no moving parts, a Peltier cooler can heat or cool by simply reversing the electrical current.
